Debate Project

Patrin Sinteppadon | Kiran Pradhan

Topic Summary

As high school friends and officers in the same student club, we wanted to put our efforts into the realm of debate. The National Forensics League (NFL) is an organization that sponsors debate events for young students with hopes of promoting educational argumentation. We believe debate to be important because the skills it trains, like research, delivery, reasoning, and logic, transfer well as important life skills.

There are many types of debate events, each with their own rulesets. In high school we were partners in Public Forum (PuFo), which emphasizes a tag-team debate style and thorough research to build an argumentative case. Another popular event was Spontaneous Argumentation (SPAR), which took a more casual approach to debate encouraging heavy audience participation.

Though we loved debate, getting our peers to join was difficult. New joiners needed to put in so much work before getting to the actual debate that most students did not want to try. Creating an app that streamlines the process to the core experience of debate creates a strong entry point for new joiners to evaluate their interest in debate club.

Pre-Existing Issues

Practically all information at local tournaments are conveyed through paper: student case notes, judge score-sheets, room placements, and next-round assignments. This keeps events slow and, because this information is often misplaced, tournament delays often build up and cause debate tournaments to run over time.

Debate is also an especially difficult activity to get into in the first place. Many of the NFL’s events involve a serious amount of preparation--Public Forum cases are often upwards of 10 pages long with over 20 sources. The reason students participate is to spend their time debating--the research itself is just seen as a necessary grind,and convincing a complete beginner to invest that time before they can even see if they enjoy it discourages a lot of potential club members.

Not only this, once members are recruited, there is a lack of beginner-friendly resources to improve at their desired event. Most students rely on the expertise of their debate club officers to learn. In our case, those debate officers only had some experience themselves, which put our school’s team at a clear disadvantage compared to other schools in the district with more established debate programs. Many students would join to explore the club, and quit when there was no clear structure for improving.

Lastly, it was difficult for students to venture out and try a new event. Since students relied on the officer's past experiences, eventually everyone in the club competed in the one same event. Resources about events online were scattered and there were few example videos, especially of newer events.

Potential Web App Solutions

With a variety of different pain points affecting the high school and college debate experience, there is more than one way our project could help the field. Included below are a few of the possible solutions we have discussed.

  1. Tournament organization tool. A tournament organizer can go to this website and create a session for their tournament. Competitors and judges can access the session and see current standings for the tournament, as well as room assignments for the next round. Each judge can be assigned a match in the form of a task--upon completion of the match, the judge will upload their scores to the web app, including notes on how the debate went. This allows the tournament organizer to evaluate scores and keep the tournament moving more smoothly. Addresses the issue of tournaments being disorganized and slow.
  2. A website with short or simple sample cases donated by schools as a public resource. This allows new debate students to immediately try their hand at debating rather than spending hours developing a case first. By evaluating whether they enjoy the sport sooner, this encourages the students to become regular members of their debate club. This would also be a tool to help new debaters to learn how to craft research cases. New debaters can study the templates and see what role each section of the content plays in the debate.
  3. Debate “game” that automates the mediation of a SPAR event. The game would generate a random topic, manage the time of each speaking segment, and prompt the audience to vote on a winner at the end. It is a portable way to practice fundamental debate skills, like style, delivery, logic, reasoning, and thinking fast on your feet. In addition, it also works as an accessible debate tool because of how little preparation is necessary for the SPAR event. Topics are casual (e.g. “Which is better: cats or dogs?”), making SPAR the most approachable debate event for new competitors.
